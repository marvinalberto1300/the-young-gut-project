What Is Colorectal Cancer?

Colorectal cancer is a disease in which cells in the colon or rectum (parts of the large intestine) grow uncontrollably, sometimes forming tumors.[1] Most colorectal cancers begin as a growth called a polyp, which can sometimes turn into cancer over time.[1]

  • The colon absorbs water and nutrients from food and stores waste.
  • The rectum is the final section before waste leaves the body.

Colorectal cancer is the third most common cancer diagnosed in both men and women in the United States.[2]

Why Are Early-Onset Cases Rising?

In recent years, there has been a noticeable increase in colorectal cancer diagnoses among adults under 50, a trend known as early-onset colorectal cancer.[3] Researchers are actively studying why this is happening, but the exact causes remain unclear.

  • Possible factors include changes in diet, physical activity, obesity rates, gut microbiome, and environmental exposures.[3][4]
  • Increased awareness and improved screening may also contribute to higher detection rates.[2]

No single factor explains all cases, and research is ongoing.

What Has Research Shown?

  • High intake of red and processed meats, low fiber diets, and excessive alcohol use are associated with increased risk.[4][5]
  • Regular physical activity, healthy weight, and not smoking are linked to lower risk.[4][5]
  • Family history and certain genetic conditions can increase risk.[1][2]

What Remains Uncertain?

  • Most people with early-onset colorectal cancer do not have a known genetic cause.[3]
  • Association does not mean causation; more research is needed to understand the reasons for rising rates.
