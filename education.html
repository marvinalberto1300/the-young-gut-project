Educational Library

This page summarizes public-health style information in plain language. It describes research themes without giving treatment advice or guarantees.

Processed and Ultra-Processed Foods

Ultra-processed foods are items with many industrial ingredients, such as flavor enhancers, stabilizers, and refined additives. Studies have explored how frequent use of these foods may be associated with broader digestive and metabolic health patterns.

Stress and Inflammation

Chronic stress has been studied in relation to inflammatory responses in the body. Research in this area looks at patterns over time and across populations, not instant cause-and-effect for a single person.

Lifestyle Patterns and Gut Health

Sleep, movement, alcohol use, and smoking are often studied alongside diet to understand overall gut-health patterns. Researchers generally describe these as interacting factors rather than one isolated cause.

Fact vs Fiction: Social Media Myths

Fiction: "A viral TikTok says coffee enemas cure colon cancer."

Fact: There is no scientific evidence that coffee enemas cure or prevent colorectal cancer. In fact, they can be dangerous and cause serious harm. [1][2]

Fiction: "Facebook posts claim eating only alkaline foods prevents all cancers."

Fact: No reputable health organization supports the idea that an "alkaline diet" can prevent or cure cancer. Balanced nutrition is important, but cancer is complex and not caused by a single food or pH level. [1][2][4]

Fiction: "Supplements advertised on social media can replace screening tests."

Fact: No supplement can replace evidence-based screening like colonoscopy or stool tests. Early detection saves lives. [1][3][4]

Fiction: "If you have no symptoms, you don't need to worry about colorectal cancer."

Fact: Colorectal cancer can develop with no symptoms in early stages. Regular screening is recommended for adults starting at age 45, or earlier if you have risk factors. [1][2][3]

What the Research Does Not Say

Current evidence does not support certainty for individual outcomes, guaranteed prevention, or a single lifestyle formula that applies to everyone.

Research language is about probabilities and associations at group level, and findings continue to evolve over time.
